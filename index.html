About Me

Educator, Researcher,
Lifelong Learner

I believe that education is not merely the transfer of knowledge — it is the cultivation of curiosity, resilience, and the lifelong desire to grow.

Over 15 years of teaching English, and a decade specialising in IELTS preparation, I have worked with learners from all walks of life — school students finding their voice, university undergraduates navigating academic English for the first time, and working professionals who need the language to open the next door in their careers. Each of them has taught me as much as I have taught them.

That curiosity about learning eventually led me to research. I hold a Master's degree in English Linguistics, and I am currently a Doctoral Candidate in Education at Edgewood University. My research sits at an intersection I find genuinely exciting: the integration of Artificial Intelligence into Writing instruction — and what that reveals about how we should design pedagogy when technology enters the language classroom.

Beyond the classroom and the research desk, I am the founder of The Becoming Learners — a bilingual YouTube channel where I share reflections on education, language learning, and the ongoing process of growth. Because good ideas about learning should not be locked behind a language barrier.

Research

Doctoral Research

My research sits at the intersection of language education, artificial intelligence, and cognitive development.

Edgewood University · Doctoral Programme in Education

The Integration of AI into English Writing Instruction: Exploring Critical and Creative Thinking in Asynchronous Online Learning

This research investigates how AI tools shape the emergence and development of critical and creative thinking in learners engaged in English Writing tasks — specifically within asynchronous online learning environments delivered through a Learning Management System (LMS).

RQ 01

How do critical and creative thinking emerge and develop in the process of AI-assisted English Writing in asynchronous online learning?

RQ 02

What role does the asynchronous LMS environment play in shaping learner cognitive engagement with AI writing tools?

RQ 03

What pedagogical design principles best support meaningful thinking development when AI is integrated into the writing classroom?

📄
Qualitative & Mixed-Methods
Think-aloud protocols, reflective journals, and learner writing samples analysed for evidence of critical and creative thinking processes.
💻
LMS Interaction Data
Analysis of learner behaviour within asynchronous online environments — tracking engagement patterns, AI usage, and writing development over time.
👤
Learner-Centred Design
Real learners in real online learning contexts — prioritising ecological validity and practical applicability alongside academic rigour.
⏱
Longitudinal Approach
Tracking the development of thinking processes across multiple writing tasks and time points to capture change and growth over the course of the study.

The Bigger Picture

AI is already in our classrooms — not as a future possibility, but as a present reality that learners navigate every day. The question is no longer whether to engage with it, but how to do so in ways that deepen rather than diminish human thinking.

This research aims to contribute a principled, evidence-based answer — one that serves both educators designing courses and learners trying to grow. Research in progress · Findings will be shared as the work develops.

Teaching

Learner-Centred
Practice

Teaching, for me, has never been simply about content delivery. It is about building the conditions in which learners can grow.

I believe that every learner has the capacity to acquire language when given the right environment, tools, and encouragement. My approach is learner-centred — I begin by understanding who my students are, what they already know, and where they want to go.

For IELTS preparation, I do not teach to the test alone. I teach learners to think critically, communicate clearly, and engage confidently with academic and professional English. The exam score is a milestone, not the destination.

I draw on research in second language acquisition, cognitive psychology, and reflective practice to continually refine my methods — ensuring that what I bring into the classroom is grounded in both theory and lived experience.
